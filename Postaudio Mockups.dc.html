Dad’s Story · with Anna topic 2 of 4 12:41
Anna

Let’s stay in the bakery a while. You were fourteen when you started under your uncle Cor. What kind of boss was he?

Henk

Strict. Not unkind — strict. If a loaf came out wrong he’d tap the bench twice with his knuckle. He never shouted, not once in eleven years. The tap was worse.

Anna

Worse how? What did those two taps mean to you, standing there at fourteen?

Henk

That he had expected better of you. Shouting is for strangers. The tap meant you were family, and you knew it.

Anna

What we heard

Henk talked about the objects his father left behind — the shop clock, the watch, and why he keeps both stopped.

  • The shop clock hung above the door to the back of the bakery and was wound every Saturday after closing — same key, same ritual, for forty years.
  • His father’s Sunday watch went unworn for decades; Henk still winds it every week because letting it stop feels wrong.
  • When his father died, the bakery ledgers went to Willem — the clock and the watch came to Henk.

Transcript
Anna

You mentioned the shop clock earlier. Tell me about it — where did it hang?

Henk

Above the door to the back, so you saw it every time you carried the trays through. My father wound it every Saturday after closing. Same ritual, same little key on the same nail.

Anna

And the watch — is that the same story?

Henk

The watch he wore only on Sundays. It came to me with the clock. I can’t wear it — my wrist is too wide — but I wind it anyway. You don’t let another man’s time run out.
